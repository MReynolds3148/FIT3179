GLOBAL TRAFFICKING

With technological improvements making travel consistently easier and cheaper, and wealth disparities creating a bigger and bigger divide between classes, forcing some people to turn to smuggling and illegal trafficking to survive and prosper.

Although the globe is more peaceful than ever, wars still rage and altercations still occur daily. This creates a demand for weapons, espcially small arms, which often are more easily and quickly obtained through less-than-legal suppliers, especially for countries with strong firearm laws.

Throughout history people have also been treated as a commodity for the wealthy, being used as servants or slaves. This trend appears to have continued into the modern age, as human trafficking is still a large issue.

When illegal firearms are seized or human trafficking victims are rescued, the occurence is often reported to the United Nations. The UN makes this data open-source, and tracks parameters such as location, what the outcome of raids were, and victim nationality. The currently available data contains information from 2010-2020 for firearm seizure, and 2007-2017 for human trafficking rescues. This report aims to compare the amount of incidents between different countries and regions for these issues over the 10 year period.

It must be noted that this report makes no conclusions about the relative safety of different countries based on this data, as instances must be reported to the UN for tracking. A country may not disclose all this information, and so smuggling rates may be higher than they appear. Also, a country with stricter laws and more resources will be able to make more successful raids, leading to an apparent increase relative to countries with less resources and less means of controlling dangerous imports.
